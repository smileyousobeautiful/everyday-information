The psychology of anger: understanding and management

## 📄 文章
Anger is one of the most powerful emotions. It can motivate change, set boundaries, and protect us from harm. But when uncontrolled, it destroys relationships, damages health, and leads to regret.

Understanding anger:

Anger exists on a spectrum—from frustration to irritation to rage. The key is recognizing where you are on this spectrum.

Anger has three components: physiological (increased heart rate, muscle tension), cognitive ("This is unfair!"), and behavioral (voice raising, aggression).

Common triggers include: feeling disrespected, threatened, or controlled; having goals blocked; experiencing injustice; physical discomfort (hunger, fatigue, pain).

Why anger spirals:

There's a phenomenon called "anger rumination." Once angry, we mentally replay the offense, making it worse. We also tend to "catastrophize"—turning a small offense into a major injustice.

The "hit" theory suggests that anger temporarily feels good—it releases tension and provides energy. This makes it addictive.

How to manage anger:

First, recognize early signs. Notice physical cues—clenched jaw, racing heart. The earlier you catch it, the easier to manage.

Second, use "time-out." Remove yourself from the situation. "I need a 20-minute break before we continue this conversation."

Third, cognitive reappraisal. Ask: "Is there another way to see this?" "What might explain their behavior?"

Fourth, breathe. Deep breathing activates the parasympathetic nervous system, calming the fight-or-flight response.

Fifth, express assertively, not aggressively. "I feel frustrated when... because... I need..."

Sixth, exercise. Physical activity provides an outlet for the physiological arousal.

## 🔄 逆向视角
Anger isn't always bad. Appropriate anger motivates change, sets boundaries, and addresses injustice. The goal isn't to eliminate anger but to express it constructively.
