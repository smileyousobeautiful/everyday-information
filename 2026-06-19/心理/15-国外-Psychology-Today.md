## 💡 核心观点
Our financial decisions are more driven by psychology than logic. Understanding biases like loss aversion, present bias, and mental accounting can help us make better money choices.

## 📖 背景信息
Behavioral economist Richard Thaler won the Nobel Prize for his work on "nudge" economics. Research shows that people systematically make irrational financial decisions—from overspending to undersaving to falling for scams. Understanding these biases is the first step to overcoming them.

## 📄 文章
Why do smart people make dumb money decisions? Why do you know you should save but can't seem to do it? Why do sales work so well on you, even when you know they're manipulation?

The answer is: money decisions aren't purely rational. They're deeply psychological.

Nobel Prize-winning economist Richard Thaler showed that we systematically make decisions that aren't in our best interest. Here are the key psychological biases:

LOSS AVERSION: losing $100 hurts twice as much as winning $100 feels good. This is why we hold onto losing stocks hoping to "break even," or why we can't pull the trigger on good investments.

PRESENT BIAS: we value the present over the future, even when the future is more important. This is why we overspend now and undersave for retirement. The "future us" feels like a stranger.

MENTAL ACCOUNTING: we treat money differently depending on where it comes from or where it's stored. A $100 bonus feels like "free money" to spend, while our salary feels more "real." This is irrational—a dollar is a dollar.

ANCHORING: we rely too heavily on the first piece of information. If a stock was once $100, we think "expensive" at $80—even if it's now fairly priced.

SOCIAL PROOF: we look to others to guide our decisions. We buy what others buy, invest where others invest. This can be good (wisdom of crowds) or bad (following crowds off cliffs).

CONFIRMATION BIAS: we seek information that supports our existing beliefs. If we think "crypto is a scam," we only read articles about crypto failures.

How to overcome these biases:

First, automate your savings. Remove willpower from the equation.

Second, make saving visible. "Out of sight, out of mind" works against you.

Third, use commitment devices. Lock away money you can't touch.

Fourth, slow down decisions. Sleep on big purchases.

Fifth, seek contrary opinions. Ask someone who disagrees with your investment thesis.
