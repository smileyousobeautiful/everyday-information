## 📄 文章
Many people feel guilty about taking time for themselves. We live in a culture that glorifies being busy, sacrifice, and putting others first. But psychology research shows that neglecting self-care is not noble—it's harmful, both psychologically and physiologically.

The concept of self-care originated in medical settings, particularly in the HIV/AIDS crisis when patients needed to take active roles in managing their health. Since then, research has expanded our understanding dramatically.

From a neurological perspective, chronic self-neglect activates the body's stress response system. When we're constantly in "giving mode" without replenishing ourselves, the hypothalamus-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis stays activated. Over time, this leads to elevated cortisol levels, which are associated with anxiety, depression, immune dysfunction, and even cardiovascular problems.

Self-care isn't just bubble baths and spa days (though those can be part of it!). Psychologists categorize self-care into several types:

Physical self-care: sleep, exercise, nutrition, medical care
Emotional self-care: processing feelings, therapy, creative expression
Social self-care: maintaining relationships, setting boundaries
Spiritual self-care: meaning, purpose, connection
Professional self-care: career satisfaction, work-life balance

The key principle is that self-care must be intentional and regular—not just when we're exhausted. Think of it like maintaining a car: you don't wait for the engine to seize before changing the oil.

Practical strategies include: scheduling self-care like appointments, starting small, getting accountability partners, and reframing self-care as professional responsibility rather than indulgence.
