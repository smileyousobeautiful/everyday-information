New Blood Pressure Guidelines: Why the Lower Threshold Matters

## 📄 文章
The American Heart Association updated diagnostic thresholds, catching millions off guard. Previously considered "pre-hypertensive" now qualifies as hypertension - but this shift represents better science, not manufactured epidemic.

Threshold changes follow extensive research. Lower values predict elevated cardiovascular risk even above previously-treated levels. Analysis showed treating to lower targets prevents more heart attacks, strokes, and death. The cost of wider treatment is outweighed by prevented complications.

Numbers tell the story: Normal is below 120/80. Elevated is 120-129/<80. Stage 1 hypertension is 130-139/80-89. Stage 2 is 140+/90+. Stage 1 with high cardiovascular risk qualifies for medication; stage 1 without typically begins with 3-6 months lifestyle modification.

Lifestyle interventions work. DASH diet ( Mediterranean-style sodium reduction ) consistently lowers 10-15 mmHg systolic. Regular moderate exercise achieves 5-8 mmHg reduction. Weight loss of 10+ pounds impacts 5-20 mmHg. Limiting alcohol and managing stress add additional benefits.

Monitoring accuracy matters. White coat syndrome causes elevated readings in clinical settings. Home BP monitoring ( morning and evening readings averaged over a week ) gives more reliable baseline. Automated cuff devices perform adequately if validated and properly sized.

Treatment expectations. Starting medications requires patience. Most require 2-4 weeks for full effect. Combinations often necessary. Side effect management ( cough with ACE inhibitors, swelling with calcium channel blockers ) allows switching rather than suffering through.

The key insight: elevated BP is silent damage accumulating over years. Earlier detection enables earlier intervention, preventing the heart attacks and strokes that declare these problems too late.
